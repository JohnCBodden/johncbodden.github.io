Cyber Security Explained

Throughout human history, security, in one form or another, has played a pertinent role in the advancement and survival of mankind. Historically, security has centered on the safety and fortification of physical aspects of our existence—such as our families, our homes, our bodies, and our countries. In recent times, a new form of security concern has emerged with the wide-spread use of technology. Despite technology being conceived with the intention of enhancing global communication, it also poses various threats to our lives, businesses and countries if it is used by individuals with malicious intents.

We acknowledge the convenience and enhanced standard of living provided by a number of technological modifications to our traditional everyday necessities of 21st century living--such as, the introduction of ‘devices’ - namely computers, the smartphone and other ‘smart’ devices ranging from the refrigerator to a water bottle that alerts us when it is time to rehydrate. Technological advancement has brought with it handiness, and increased vulnerabilities. These devices integrate technologies which allow them to connect and communicate with each other via the internet, ultimately providing each of use with never–before-experienced means of communication, the sharing of information, the management of our everyday tasks and other [previously] futuristic conveniences. In order to effectively perform their specified functions, such devices are typically remotely accessible and controlled from just about anywhere in the world where an internet connection is available. Despite this being commendable technological advancement - here is where the risk lies.

These remotely accessible capabilities are what create opportunities for anyone with the requisite skill and intention to breach or access those communication paths in an effort to control, manage, intercept, alter, damage or withhold the often vital data stored by, or accessed from, any such device. With these looming and ever-increasing threats, the rise of cyber security protocol within homes and organisations has begun to take front and center of the technology realm. In order to mitigate risk, businesses and individuals alike have begun to adopt a variety of a cyber security practices in an attempt to ward-off potential attacks – after-all, “an ounce of prevention is better than a pound of cure” – or potentially, several million dollars of cure.

Cyber security has been defined as “the protection of information/data, assets, services, and systems of value to reduce the probability of loss, damage/corruption, compromise, or misuse to a level commensurate with the value assigned.” (Craigen, et al., 2014)

In very recent years, businesses have become more cognizant of the reputational and pecuniary damage that can be experienced with an inadvertent breach of their information technology infrastructure. This widespread realization has greatly increased the need for cyber security professionals who are qualified, experienced and knowledgeable in the field of cyber security as more organizations seek to employ or contract specialist to oversee this vital area of their operations.

As the cyber security area of the information technology industry grows and develops, it is necessary for cyber security professionals to set themselves apart and remain abreast their field – and ahead of any potential cyber criminals in order to protect everyday users, businesses and government organisations. One of the ways that information technology professionals can achieve this would be through higher learning. Despite having decades of information technology experience and a proven track record of the ability to create secure networks and frameworks, many organizations will still overlook a professional candidate’s resume and portfolio if there is another candidate who has attained a greater standard of educational achievement; particularly, if that educational achievement is at postgraduate level.

A postgraduate degree in Cyber Security not only exhibits an individual’s in-depth understanding of information security management and is evidence of an individual’s dedication to their chosen field. Undergoing a postgraduate degree program also helps one to become a more competitive player through specialization, and provides for more comprehensive knowledge in the specific area – in this case being, cyber security.

It is a common misconception amongst everyday technology users that cyber security is reserved for large corporations and their professional hires. However, a cyber security breach aimed at your local bank, for example, can have detrimental impact on families, employees and the community at large should vital customer and operational data get in to the wrong hands. It has been estimated that fifty percent of small businesses in the United States of America that experience a cyberattack cease to operate within six months as a direct consequence of the attack. (Testimony of Dr. Jane LeClair, 2015) This is evidence that cyber breaches have the potential to cause the complete collapse of organisations which can leave hundreds or thousands unemployed, in turn creating further issues. The risk of cyber threats can be better mitigated with sufficient numbers of qualified and dedicated professionals dispatched through the cyber security world predominately for the purpose of fortifying vital information security infrastructures and ultimately safeguarding the continuity of organisations and the individuals they service and/or employ.

Glossary

Packet-Filtering

This type of firewall creates a checkpoint for traffic coming through a router or gateway, which is a device that forwards the data packets between networks. It then performs a simple check of the data packets coming through the gateway device. As the data packet contains information, including the IP addresses of the originating and destination computers, it can determine the validity of the packet. Invalid packets are then dropped. This firewall technique is considered the oldest type.

Circuit-level Gateways

This type of network operates by simply approving or denying access. This happens through a process known as TCP handshake. TCP handshake is a verification process which ensures that the data packet is from a valid source and therefore legitimate. Again, because the data packet contains what is supposed to be valid information (the firewall can determine this validity) when the check reveals that data packet contains invalid information, then it is considered illegitimate.

Stateful Inspection

This type of firewall tends to combine the TCP handshake verification and packet inspection approaches described earlier to create a more secure firewall. One of the downsides of this type of approach to the firewall is that it tends to slow down the network because it must carry out this check on each data packet.

Software Firewall

This is concerned with any type of firewall installed on a host computer. It is highly useful as it allows users to be able to isolate networks or isolate their system from a network. However, maintenance can be a challenge and time consuming as it must be done on individual host computers.

Hardware Firewall

This type of firewall uses a physical device, like the gateway, which intercepts packets before reaching the network. It ensures malicious attacks from outside the network are intercepted and do not leave the institution's network exposed. It is, however, easy to bypass by a malicious attacker inside the institution.

Cloud Firewall

This is when the firewall is deployed as a cloud solution. It is sometimes called Firewall-as-a-Service (FaaS). Unlike software firewalls, this type of firewall is scalable and easy to maintain.
