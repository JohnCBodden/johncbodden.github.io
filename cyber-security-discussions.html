Cyber Security Discussion and Understanding

It has become customary for many organizations to purchase third-party software and applications for monitoring and keeping their network infrastructure secure. Whether as ‘software-as-a-service’ or on-premise; these organisations all trust their vendors to provide confidential, effective and efficient information technology security support and updates to maintain their own network’s unassailability. In order for these relationships to remain mutually beneficial, the onus primarily lies with the vendor to ensure its network management systems are at the highest impenetrable level possible to avoid potential breaches being passed on to clients.

In what has been reported as one of the most advanced cyber security hacks of 2020, and possibly the 21st century; SolarWinds, a major software company providing network management services to thousands of companies around the world, including government agencies, found itself in a precarious situation where which its client’s vital data had been compromised by threat actors who gained access by first penetrating SolarWinds’ own software with injected code creating a backdoor entry to SolarWinds’ clients’ networks. This breach was not discovered until over one year after the systems had been infiltrated and the overall cost to SolarWinds has been estimated at $40 million (Schwartz, 2021), coupled with the effects of reputational damage and exposure of client data to an unknown third party.

The potential of exponential fiscal and reputational damage to businesses, governments and individuals alike by way of inadvertent malicious cyber breaches, has caused laser-like focus on Cyber Security worldwide. There is a great deal of verified examples of cyber breaches occurring within the past two decades which highlight the often-irreversible damage which can be experienced if consistent effort to maintain network security is not made a paramount endeavour within organisations globally.

Peer Response - Emmon

Thanks Emmon, it is absolutely correct that cyber threats have evolved in the last two decades as described in your post. The more recent examples of cyber security attacks which have taken place within the last five years demonstrate how complex modern-day attacks have become. As you have raised in your example, these attacks are highly strategic, have a wide-ranging multi-national affect and can cost tens of millions of dollars in losses; in addition to the usual reputational damage and penalties.

The Swift system is one that many of us have utilized if we have every had to send funds internationally from one bank to another. Everyday users of the system rarely consider the myriad of cyber security provisions that must be implemented to secure their transactions, such as the SWIFT Customer Security Controls Framework (CSCF) that you have mentioned. The CSCF has three overarching objectives that are mandatory and recommended for all SWIFT users to implement into their infrastructure to establish a secure baseline. SWIFT defines these objectives as (a) Secure your Environment, (b) Know and Limit Access and (c) Detect and Respond. (Swift - The global provider of secure financial messaging services, n.d.) SWIFT provides product-specific Security Guidance to its users to ensure that the three objectives have been met with their Security Controls Framework and in line with existing information security industry standards. For example, with the ‘Secure your Environment’ objective; the four basic principles defined by SWIFT are:

  1. Restrict internet access
  2. Segregate critical systems from general IT environment
  3. Reduce attack surface and vulnerabilities; and
  4. Physically secure the environment.

SWIFT’s implementation of its CSCF after suffering the breaches is evidence that organisations are setting cyber security on a pinnacle and thoroughly recognize the important of a comprehensive cyber security plan.

Summary Post

The SolarWinds data breach, which infected nearly 1800 of its customers, is said to have been caused by a software supply chain attack (Lakshmanan, 2020), outlined very nicely by Steve Fisher in his peer response. The cybercriminals installed backdoor software known as malware in the SolarWinds Orion products that have been denizen for nearly one year before being detected. The attack was one of the most sophisticated methods used by cybercriminals to penetrate a list of government agencies, including the entire US military and Fortune 500 companies.

Supply chain attacks are said to have grown by 300% in 2021 compared to 2020 in research made by Argon Security (Elhadad, 2021). According to Help Net Security, 32% of organizations continue to trust software supply chain open-source repositories. In comparison, 82% of CIOs are concerned that cyber-attacks targeting software supply chains would cause business disruptions, loss of revenue, data breaches caused by theft and customer damage for industries which rely on open-source repositories are vulnerable to cyberattacks. (Help Net Security, 2022)

In his initial post, Steven Fisher asked, "Were there any opportunities missed which would have enabled the hack to be spotted earlier than it was?" An option would have been to use a configured firewall to block outbound traffic to the internet; the firewall configured to block the connection made by the malware would have contained and prevented the attack on the SolarWinds products and its customers' network infrastructure. (Security Magazine, 2021)

The peer response made by Stephen Agupusi states that organizations, even with state-of-the-art cybersecurity frameworks in-place, should consider their network infrastructure vulnerable to attacks, which is an agreeable statement. Additional security measures to monitor cyber threats and implement security policies and controls would dramatically reduce an organization's attack surface from further cyberattacks.
